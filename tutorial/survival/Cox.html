Semi-parametrics methods

Cox model

Cox's model [1] can be found a lot in the literature. Primrose et al [2] use it for example to study the effect of the adjuvant capecitabine on patient survival after cholangiocarcinoma or gallbladder cancer resection surgery. It is used when the objective is to evaluate the effect of covariates on survival time. It allows the occurrence of a qualitative event to be explained over time by one or more explanatory variables (univariate and multivariate analysis respectively) which may be qualitative or quantitative. For each of the variables present in the final model, we obtain an estimate of the relative risk (hazard ratio) of an event occurring in the presence of the variable, and its confidence interval. The hazard ratio is equal to The hazard ratio is equal to the instantaneous relative risk of the event adjusted for all the explanatory variables introduced into the model. This implies the assumption that the risk of death in the different study groups is constant over time and similar in all subgroups. A basic hazard function is introduced that gives the general form of hazard and is common to all individuals. We introduce a basic hazard function h characterized by the relation below :

Let an individual i, for any t > 0,

$$h(t|X_{i}) = h_{0}(t) x f(β,X_{i}) $$

with:

  • Xi = (Xi1,...,Xip) a vector of p-dimensional covariates of the individual i (the covariates being individual-specific variables that may influence the output of the model),
  • β the parameter of interest (does not depend on time t) of dimension p, which represents the effect of the covariates on instantaneous risk
  • f a positive function of hazard.

This model is said to have proportional risks because, whatever two individuals i and j whose covariates are the vectors Xi and Xj, the ratio of the hazard functions does not vary over time. $$ \frac{λ(t| X_{i})}{λ(t|X_{j})} = \frac{h(\beta*X_{i})}{h(\beta*X_{j})} $$ In the case of the Cox model, the function f is an exponential function, thus equation becomes : $$ λ(t|(X_{i1}, ... X_{ip}) = λ_{0}(t)e^{β_{1}*X_{i1} + ... + β_{p}*X_{ip}}$$

The principle is to estimate β. It is obtained by the maximum likelihood method, and more particularly the partial likelihood method which includes information on the coefficients βi [3].
